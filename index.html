Introduction

Within the United States Air Force, there exists a fleet of fighter jets that display engineering and technology of different generations. The class of fighter jets containing the most advanced and cutting-edge technology/engineering are known as the fifth-generation fighters. These jets are “capable of operating effectively in highly contested combat environments” against the “presence of the most capable current air and ground threats” (Harrigian and Morosko, 2017). Currently in operation fifth-generation aircraft include the F-35B Lightning II, the main subject of this webpage, and the F-22A Raptor, each aircraft belonging to the US Marine Corps and the Air Force respectively. Just to go into some detail, there are many characteristics of these aircraft that clearly distinguish them from the older aircraft: “multi-spectral low observable (LO) design features” make detection even more difficult with even the most advanced detection technology, “integrated avionics”(Harrigian and Morosko, 2017) which simultaneously manages several systems and data to provide real time communication and aerial dominance, although some fourth-generation fighters have thrust vectoring most fifth-generation aircraft improve the use of it to achieve supermanoeuvrability. These are just some of the key characteristics to be named, which will be further explained in detail in the analysis and discussion section. The F35b lightning is arguably the most advanced fighter jet up to date and is the latest operational American fighter jet. It’s classified as a fifth-generation aircraft capable of climbing to speeds of Mach 1.6 (Air, 2024), which is 1.6 times the speed of sound! It’s equipped with a single engine and STOVL (Short Take Off and Vertical Landing) system which allows the aircraft to take off from short runaways and land like a helicopter, this is why you’ll see the U.S. Marine Corps utilising them as they can operate from assault ships despite the short runway length.

Analysis and discussion

The STOVL system

As previously mentioned, the F35B is equipped with a STOVL system. It achieves this by separating the single nozzle into three rotating segments collectively called the “three bearing swivel module” (3BSM). This allows for a 45° and then a 95° vertical rotation of the thrust direction as shown in Figure 1, this allows for short runway take off as lift and thrust can now be simultaneously produced.

A single “conventional gas turbine” passes 18,000 lbf of thrust into the 3BSM, redirecting it to a 95 degree angle in 2.5 seconds. Another addition to this STOVL system is the Rolls-Royce LiftFan, a “two-stage counter-rotating fan" capable of producing more than “20000 lbf of thrust” (Rolls-Royce, 2023) which amounts to around 9000kg or 9 tonnes, roughly the force generated from the weight of a large truck on earth. This thrust is achieved from a driveshaft that transmits the torque and rotation of up to 29,000 horsepower from the Pratt & Whitney F135 engine (Rolls-Royce, n.d.) which in turn spins the central LiftFan’s bevel gear, ultimately spinning the blades to generate vertical thrust. 29,000 horse power is the equivalent of 21.7 megawatts of power, it would take roughly 58 standard cars each of 500 HP to equal the power of 29,000 HP. Finally, the system includes 2 additional Roll posts on each side of the wings that harness power from the engine to “provide a further 1,950 lbf of thrust each”. This helps stabilize the aircraft in hover mode, in other words aid in the aircraft’s roll (side to side) axis during vertical flight modes. These three components work in unison to make up the STOVL system as shown in Figure 2 below.

Stealth Combat

When it comes to stealth combat, many seem to have the impression that the aircraft must be completely invisible to detection, that would be the case in an ideal world however realistically, the objective is to detect the enemy before they detect you. This is achieved by minimising the difference between a return signal of background noise or an enemy aircraft for the enemy radar receiver, making it harder to distinguish which buys time. When it comes to radar detection, specular reflection (where the angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection of the radar wave) is the main principle that lets radar detection thrive so undoubtedly, the F35B contains designs that prevent specular reflection wherever possible. Since specular reflection occurs on smooth flat surfaces (Warner and Jardine-Wright, 2025), many of the aircraft’s design features serrated edges and angled surfaces, this is why you see a v-tail configuration instead of the usual vertical tail. You’d think this would harm the manoeuvrability of the aircraft however this configuration actually aids in lift and yaw as shown in Figure 3. These choices in design are known to be called low-observable stealth technology
