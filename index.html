Introduction

Within the United States Air Force, there exists a fleet of fighter jets that display engineering and technology of different generations. The class of fighter jets containing the most advanced and cutting-edge technology/engineering are known as the fifth-generation fighters. These jets are “capable of operating effectively in highly contested combat environments” against the “presence of the most capable current air and ground threats” (Harrigian and Morosko, 2017). Currently in operation fifth-generation aircraft include the F-35B Lightning II, the main subject of this webpage, and the F-22A Raptor, each aircraft belonging to the US Marine Corps and the Air Force respectively. Just to go into some detail, there are many characteristics of these aircraft that clearly distinguish them from the older aircraft: “multi-spectral low observable (LO) design features” make detection even more difficult with even the most advanced detection technology, “integrated avionics”(Harrigian and Morosko, 2017) which simultaneously manages several systems and data to provide real time communication and aerial dominance, although some fourth-generation fighters have thrust vectoring most fifth-generation aircraft improve the use of it to achieve supermanoeuvrability. These are just some of the key characteristics to be named, which will be further explained in detail in the analysis and discussion section. The F35b lightning is arguably the most advanced fighter jet up to date and is the latest operational American fighter jet. It’s classified as a fifth-generation aircraft capable of climbing to speeds of Mach 1.6 (Air, 2024), which is 1.6 times the speed of sound! It’s equipped with a single engine and VTOL (Vertical Take Off and Landing) system which allows the aircraft to take off from short runaways and land like a helicopter, this is why you’ll see the U.S. Marine Corps utilising them as they can operate from assault ships despite the short runway length.

Analysis and discussion

The VTOL system

As previously mentioned, the F35B is equipped with a VTOL system. It achieves this by separating the single nozzle into three rotating segments collectively called the “three bearing swivel module” (3BSM). This allows for a 45° and then a 95° vertical rotation of the thrust direction as shown in Figure 1.

A single “conventional gas turbine” passes 18,000 lbf of thrust into the 3BSM, redirecting it to a 95 degree angle in 2.5 seconds. Another addition to this VTOL system is the Rolls-Royce LiftFan, a “two-stage counter-rotating fan capable of producing more than “20000 lbf of thrust” (Rolls-Royce, 2023) which amounts to around 9000kg or 9 tonnes, roughly the force generated from the weight of a large truck on earth. This thrust is achieved from a driveshaft that transmits the torque and rotation from the Pratt & Whitney F135 engine (Rolls-Royce, n.d.) which in turn spins the central LiftFan’s bevel gear, ultimately spinning the blades to generate vertical thrust.
