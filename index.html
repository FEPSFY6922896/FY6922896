Abstract

In this webpage, I gloss over some details that the F-35b Lightning-II fighter jet employs to achieve its incredible performance in modern aerial combat, divided under 2 headings. In the introduction, I provide context for the aircraft's background and define terminology used within the United States Air Force, introducing but not going into detail about features the F-35B contains. In the analysis and discussion section, I go into detail about components of the F-35B briefly mentioned in the introduction section to gain a decent understanding of 3 key features of the aircraft: the STOVL system, electronic warfare capabilities and stealth combat features.

Introduction

Within the United States Air Force, there exists a fleet of fighter jets that display engineering and technology of different generations. The class of fighter jets containing the most advanced and cutting-edge technology/engineering are known as the fifth-generation fighters. These jets are “capable of operating effectively in highly contested combat environments” against the “presence of the most capable current air and ground threats” (Harrigian and Morosko, 2017). Currently in operation, fifth-generation aircraft include the F-35B Lightning II and the F-22A Raptor, each aircraft belonging to the US Marine Corps and the Air Force, respectively. Just to go into some detail, many characteristics of these aircraft clearly distinguish them from the older aircraft: “multi-spectral low observable (LO) design features” make detection even more difficult with even the most advanced detection technology, “integrated avionics”(Harrigian and Morosko, 2017) which simultaneously manages several systems and data to provide real time communication and aerial dominance. These are just some of the key characteristics to be named, which will be further explained in detail in the analysis and discussion section. The F-35b lightning is arguably the most advanced fighter jet to date and is the latest operational American fighter jet. It’s classified as a fifth-generation aircraft capable of climbing to speeds of Mach 1.6 (Air, 2024), which is 1.6 times the speed of sound! It’s equipped a STOVL (Short Take Off and Vertical Landing) system.

Analysis and discussion

The STOVL system

As previously mentioned, the F-35B is equipped with a STOVL system. It achieves this by separating the single nozzle into three rotating segments collectively called the “three bearing swivel module” (3BSM). This allows for a 45° and then a 95° vertical rotation of the thrust direction, as shown in Figure 1. This allows for short runway take off as lift and thrust can now be simultaneously produced.

A single “conventional gas turbine” passes 18,000 lbf of thrust into the 3BSM, redirecting it to a 95 degree angle in 2.5 seconds. Another addition to this STOVL system is the Rolls-Royce LiftFan, a “two-stage counter-rotating fan" capable of producing more than “20000 lbf of thrust” (Rolls-Royce, 2023), which amounts to around 9000kg or 9 tonnes, roughly the force generated from the weight of a large truck on earth. This thrust is achieved from a driveshaft that transmits the torque and rotation of up to 29,000 horsepower from the Pratt & Whitney F135 engine (Rolls-Royce, n.d.), which in turn spins the central LiftFan’s bevel gear, ultimately spinning the blades to generate vertical thrust. 29,000 horsepower is the equivalent of 21.7 megawatts of power; it would take roughly 58 standard cars each of 500 HP to equal the power of 29,000 HP. Finally, the system includes 2 additional Roll posts on each side of the wings that harness power from the engine to “provide a further 1,950 lbf of thrust each”. This helps stabilize the aircraft in hover mode, in other words, aid in the aircraft’s roll (side to side) axis during vertical flight modes. These three components work in unison to make up the STOVL system, as shown in Figure 2 below, which allows the aircraft to take off from short runways and land like a helicopter. This is why you’ll see the U.S. Marine Corps utilising them, as they can operate from assault ships despite the short runway length.

Electronic Warfare

The F-35B is known for its unparalleled situational awareness due to the large quantity of data the pilot is given in real time. Pilots operating the F-35B are equipped with a helmet-mounted display system, something unique to only the F-35-B which is fed information from many systems. Just below the cockpit of the jet is a small, sapphire window thats integrated into the fuselage (body of the jet). inside this window is the electrical optical targetting system (EOTS), a "high magnification thermal imager" that performs "tracking and imaging functions" (SDLinfo, 2018) to both air to air targets and air to surface targets. This sensor utilises a range of electromagnetic wavelength to provide imagery which is why it is comprised of sapphire, a hard, durable but also see-through to a range of wavelengths of em waves. Next is the DAS (Distributed Aperture System) which is a set of 6 infrared cameras mounted around the aircraft, this allows the pilot to see 360 degree view through the fuselage in xray and nightvision too as shown in Figure 4, This plays nicely with AESA (Active Electronically Scanned Array) radar.

Untraditionally, AESA has the ability to track aircraftt that is not directly in front of the aircraft, this is due to the radar waves being electronically steered via constructive and destructive interference rather than mechanically steered like traditional radars.

Stealth Combat

When it comes to stealth combat, many seem to have the impression that the aircraft must be completely invisible to detection, that would be the case in an ideal world however realistically, the objective is to detect the enemy before they detect you. This is achieved by minimising the difference between a return signal of background noise or an enemy aircraft for the enemy radar receiver, making it harder to distinguish. Since specular reflection (where the angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection of the radar wave) occurs on smooth flat surfaces (Warner and Jardine-Wright, 2025), many of the aircraft’s design features serrated edges and angled surfaces, this is why you see a v-tail configuration instead of the usual vertical tail. This doesn't harm manoeuvrability of the aircraft but actually gives equal lift and yaw as shown in Figure 3. In addition, engineers implemented radar scattering tape and radar absorbing material (RAM), the incident radar waves would be absorbed into the jet and converted to heat. These design techniques are called low-observable stealth technology.
